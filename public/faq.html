Sales tax questions, answered

Common questions about how US sales tax works in 2026: which states charge none, how to calculate tax forwards and backwards, why your receipt is higher than the state rate, and whether online orders are taxed. Rates and states

Which states have no sales tax?

Five states levy no statewide sales tax, remembered by the acronym NOMAD: New Hampshire, Oregon, Montana, Alaska, and Delaware. Delaware, Montana, New Hampshire, and Oregon allow no local sales taxes either, so the price on the tag is what you pay. Alaska is the exception, because it has no statewide tax but its cities and boroughs levy local sales taxes averaging 1.82%. See the full rates by state table for the detail.

What is the highest sales tax state?

By average combined rate, Louisiana is highest at 10.13% as of July 2026, made up of a 5% state rate plus a 5.13% average local rate. Tennessee (9.61%), Washington (9.57%), Arkansas (9.48%), and Alabama (9.46%) round out the top five.

Which state has the lowest sales tax?

Four states charge nothing at all: Delaware, Montana, New Hampshire, and Oregon. Among states that do tax sales, Alaska is lowest at a 1.82% average combined rate, which comes entirely from local taxes, followed by Hawaii at 4.5% and Wyoming at 5.39%. Colorado has the lowest statewide rate that is not zero, at 2.9%.

Calculating sales tax

How do I calculate sales tax backwards from a total?

Divide the total by 1 plus the tax rate as a decimal. If a receipt totals $108.20 and the combined rate is 8.2%, the pre-tax price is $108.20 divided by 1.082, which is $100.00, leaving $8.20 in tax. Do not multiply the total by the rate, because that overstates the tax. The calculator's reverse mode does this for you.

How do I add sales tax to a price?

Multiply the price by the combined rate as a decimal, then add it back. A $250 purchase at a combined 8.2% rate is $250 times 0.082, which is $20.50 in tax, for a total of $270.50. The calculator splits the state and local portions automatically. The full guide walks through both directions with more examples.

Local rates, online orders, and coverage

Why did I pay more than my state's sales tax rate?

Because most purchases are taxed at a combined rate, not just the state rate. Counties, cities, and special districts in 38 states add their own sales tax on top of the state rate. In Alabama, for example, the 4% state rate carries an average 5.46% in local taxes, for a 9.46% combined rate. Your receipt reflects the state rate plus every local rate that applies where you bought the item.

Do online orders get taxed?

Usually yes. Since the Supreme Court's 2018 South Dakota v. Wayfair decision, states can require out-of-state sellers to collect sales tax once their sales into the state pass an economic threshold. Large retailers collect based on your delivery address, so online orders are typically taxed at your local combined rate.

Is there a federal sales tax?

No. The United States has no national sales tax. Sales tax is entirely state and local, which is why rates range from 0% in five states to a combined average above 10% in Louisiana. Federal consumption taxes exist only as excise taxes on specific goods such as fuel, alcohol, and tobacco.

Are groceries and other essentials taxed?

It varies by state. Most states exempt unprepared groceries, many exempt prescription medicine, and a few tax them at a reduced rate. Prepared food and restaurant meals are taxable almost everywhere. This calculator applies one combined rate to the whole amount, so treat results for tax-exempt or mixed items as an approximation.
